Perfect Competition

We assume that firms:

  • are price takers;
  • aim to maximize profits;
  • use a technology that exhibits decreasing returns to scale (for the "relevant" range)—that is the MC is increasing when producing above some quantity...
$\pi(q)=p\cdot q - c(q)$

Notice that we assume constant input prices, so we write the cost function as a function of the quantity only.

To maximize profit we need that:

$\pi'(q)=\underbrace{p}_{MR}- \underbrace{c'(q)}_{MC(q)}=0 \Rightarrow MC(q)=p$

We also need the marginal cost to be increasing, so:

$q^S=MC^{-1}(p) \text{ provided that } MC'(q)>0$

is the supply decision of a firm with cost function $c(q)$.

Perfect Competition

Entry and exit: Long Run

In the previous short-run analysis, the firm might choose to produce a quantity and incur negative profits but in the long run, such firm will exit the industry.

In order for a firm to leave the industry, the market price $p$ must be such that:

$\pi(q) = p\cdot q - c(q) < 0 \text{ for all } q > 0$,

or equivalently,

$\pi(q) = \left(p - \underbrace{c(q)/q}_{ATC(q)}\right)\cdot q < 0 \text{ for all } q$,

or

$\min_q ATC(q) > p$

Perfect Competition

Entry and exit: Long Run Eq.

  • $p > \min_q ATC(q) \Rightarrow$ firms enter the industry $\Rightarrow$ industry supply curve shifts to the right (in $(p,q)$ coordinates).
  • $p < \min_q ATC(q) \Rightarrow$ firms leave the industry $\Rightarrow$ industry supply curve shifts to the left (in $(p,q)$ coordinates).

Notice that the quantity that yields the minimum of the ATC, $ATC(\underline{q})=\min_q ATC(q)$, is the one where the MC and ATC are equal: $ATC(\underline{q})=MC(\underline{q})$.

In the LR eq., $p^* = \min_q ATC(q)$.

Solving for the LR equilibrium

  1. Profit maximization condition: $MC(q)=p$
  2. Zero profit condition: $ATC(q)=p$
  3. Market eq. condition: $Q_{d}(p)=\underbrace{\underbrace{n}_{\text{# of firms}} \times \underbrace{q}_{\text{individual supply}}}_{\text{market supply}}$

Procedure:

  • Solve (1) & (2): find the equilibrium values of $p$ and $q$.
  • Plug in (3), find the equilibrium number of firms $n$ (non-integers are OK!).

Long-run eq

An Example

An individual firm cost function is:

$c(q)=q^2-2q+4$

and the market demand is:

$Q=120-2P$

Find:

  • What is the LR eq. price?
  • What is the LR eq. quantity?
  • How many firms are active in the LR?
  • How much each individual firm produces?
  • What is the consumer surplus?
  • What is the producer surplus?

Long-run eq

An Example (Solution)

The individual firm cost function and market demand are:

$c(q)=q^2-2q+4 \text{ and } Q=120-2P$

Step 1: Find minimum ATC. The min. of the ATC is reached at $q$ such that:

$ATC(q)=MC(q) \Leftrightarrow q-2+4/q=2q-2 \Leftrightarrow q=2$

So $\min ATC = ATC(2) = 2$. This is the LR eq. price: $p^* = 2$.

Step 2: Find quantities. The demand at this price is $Q^D = 120 - 2 \cdot 2 = 116$. Since in eq., $Q^S = Q^D$, the total supply in eq. must also be $116$. Since each firm supplies $q=2$ units, there must be $N = Q^S/q = 116/2 = 58$ firms operating.

Step 3: Consumer surplus (area under demand curve minus revenue):

$CS = (60-2) \cdot 116/2 = 58^2$

Step 4: Producer surplus (revenue minus variable cost). As $q=2$, the producer surplus per firm is $4-0=4$, so total producer surplus is $4 \cdot 58$.
